# The NFL's Voice of the Fan survey: a comprehensive analysis

**The NFL's Voice of the Fan (VOF) is the league's official benchmarking instrument for measuring game-day experience across all 32 clubs, and it has quietly become one of the most influential operational tools in professional sports.** Running for at least 14 seasons since 2012, the VOF surveys thousands of season ticket holders and game attendees after every home game, generating granular satisfaction data across roughly 30 subcategories — from food pricing to Wi-Fi quality to crowd energy. Stadium Technology ranks as one of its primary measurement categories, and the Tampa Bay Buccaneers have explicitly called it "the most impactful category in determining overall fan experience satisfaction." This makes the VOF a natural bridge between technology investment and measurable fan outcomes, and creates a powerful framework for correlating infrastructure maturity with satisfaction and revenue.

---

## How the Voice of the Fan works

The VOF is coordinated by the NFL league office and administered by **National Research Group (NRG)**, a third-party research firm, based on a LinkedIn job posting for a "Market Research Manager — NFL Voice of Fan" role at NRG. Survey invitations are distributed via email through **Epsilon**, the NFL's marketing database and email services partner since 2013, with surveys hosted on team-specific URLs (e.g., `surveys.chicagobears.com/s3/2019-Voice-of-the-Fan-Gameday-Survey`). Some teams incentivize completion through sweepstakes.

The survey targets fans who physically attend games — specifically **season ticket members and general game attendees** — and is deployed after every regular-season home game throughout the season. This is not a TV viewership study. Results are compiled into season-long averages, with teams ranked 1st through 32nd in each category. The NFL releases results to clubs during the offseason (typically January through March), and individual teams selectively publicize favorable rankings. The NFL does not publish the full 32-team results publicly.

Robert Gallo, NFL VP of Club Business Development, has described the program's purpose: "The Voice of the Fan survey allows us to take a very thorough analysis into the areas that really impact the in-stadium experience and to identify the best practices used by teams throughout the league." The survey includes both closed-ended rating questions (likely on a numerical scale) and open-ended comment fields. Minnesota Vikings VP Bryan Harper noted that fan comments are often more valuable than scores: "A lot of the fans will take the time to write their comments — 'This is what I like; this is what I don't like; this is what can be better.'"

## The 30+ data points VOF collects

The VOF survey has two major components measuring distinct dimensions of the fan relationship.

**Component 1: Game-Day Experience Satisfaction** spans 8–9 primary categories, each containing multiple subcategories. The primary categories are Overall Game-Day Satisfaction, Arrival, Departure, Food & Beverage, Game Entertainment, Game-Day Staff, Mobile Ticketing, Security, and Stadium Technology. The Game Entertainment category alone breaks into **10 subcategories**: overall game entertainment, audio prompts & music, crowd energy level, family-friendly entertainment, game breaks, halftime entertainment, pregame common parks/fan fests, pregame on-field entertainment, team/fan rituals, and video board content. Game-Day Staff breaks into food & beverage staff, gate entry staff, guest services staff, parking lot staff, and security staff.

Additional specific subcategories confirmed across multiple team press releases include sound system quality, "Know Before You Go" communication, **cellular service, Wi-Fi service**, rideshare pick-up, proximity of food & beverage options, food & beverage value, tailgate experience, instant replays/in-game highlights, highlights from around the NFL, and in-game & fantasy stats.

**Component 2: Season Ticket Member Satisfaction** measures the year-round relationship through categories including overall STM satisfaction, sense of being valued, exclusive benefits, **365-day experience** (year-round engagement), direction of the team, premium parking, value exceeds price, comfortable location, prestige, exclusive concessions, commitment to community, communication, and customer service quality.

The breadth of this instrument is notable. It captures the full arc of the game-day journey (arrival through departure), the in-venue environment (technology, F&B, entertainment), the service layer (staff at every touchpoint), and the ongoing season ticket holder relationship. This makes it far more than a simple satisfaction survey — it is a comprehensive diagnostic tool.

## Which teams win, and what the results reveal

While the NFL does not publish complete rankings, team press releases reveal a rich picture of VOF outcomes. The **Denver Broncos** earned #1 in Overall Game-Day Satisfaction for the 2025 season, with top marks in game entertainment, and strong finishes in crowd energy (#2), STM satisfaction (#2), stadium technology (#3), and game-day staff (#3). The **Minnesota Vikings** set an **all-time VOF record** for highest Overall Game-Day Satisfaction score in 2024, ranking #1 in game entertainment, family-friendly entertainment, team/fan rituals, and video board content. The **Detroit Lions** claimed the second-highest score in VOF history for their 2023 season, ranking #1 in overall satisfaction and crowd energy.

The most remarkable sustained performance belongs to the **Tampa Bay Buccaneers**, who have ranked #1 in Customer Service Quality for **12 consecutive seasons** through 2025 — a streak the team says produced "the league's highest rating in Voice of the Fan history" in their most recent survey. The Buccaneers have also ranked in the top-5 in stadium technology, and were previously #1 in that category.

Perhaps the most instructive VOF case study is **Mercedes-Benz Stadium** (Atlanta Falcons). After launching "Fan First" pricing in 2017 — cutting concession prices roughly 50% — the Falcons jumped from **18th in the NFL in food & beverage to #1** in the VOF. Despite the price cuts, average per-fan spending increased **16%** in year one, with **6,000 additional fans per game arriving two or more hours before kickoff**. The stadium sustained these gains in year two while improving bottom-line margins by **15%** through operational efficiencies enabled by technology: 65% more POS terminals, cashless transactions (first NFL stadium to go fully cashless in 2019), and mobile ordering integration.

## How clubs use VOF data operationally

Teams treat VOF results as actionable intelligence, not just bragging rights. The Vikings used VOF feedback to develop their "SKOL Service" philosophy (safety, courtesy, appearance, efficiency) and refine game-day production. Director Bostrom stated directly: "Looking at the Voice of the Fan metric and seeing that we were third in 2016, we said, 'We want to get to number one.'" VP Harper described the iterative process: "You keep accumulating that information, you look for trends, and those are the areas you need to improve on."

The **Baltimore Ravens** used VOF rankings to validate a **$120 million self-funded stadium investment** (2017–2019) that included 4K video displays, escalators, a new sound system, upgraded kitchens, and a redesigned club level. Following these upgrades, the Ravens ranked #1 in stadium technology and video board content. The **Denver Broncos** launched six new restaurants and 50 new menu items for the 2025 season, contributing to a #5 F&B ranking and #1 overall, while attendance climbed 9% year over year.

Teams also deploy VOF results strategically in marketing. Favorable rankings feature prominently in season ticket renewal campaigns, press releases, and stakeholder communications — transforming internal satisfaction metrics into external brand assets.

## Technology sits at the center of the VOF framework

The VOF's treatment of technology is significant. **Stadium Technology is one of only 8–9 primary measurement categories** — placing it on equal footing with food & beverage, game entertainment, and security. Technology-specific subcategories include Wi-Fi service, cellular service, video board content, sound system quality, and mobile ticketing, with additional measurement of instant replays/in-game highlights, in-game & fantasy stats, and highlights from around the NFL.

The Buccaneers' characterization of stadium technology as "the most impactful category in determining overall fan experience satisfaction" suggests the NFL's own analysis has found technology scores to be a leading predictor of overall satisfaction. This aligns with the NFL's infrastructure requirements: the league maintains **minimum Wi-Fi standards** for all venues, updated approximately every two years, specifying requirements for simultaneous users, upload/download speeds, and DAS/Wi-Fi integration. **Extreme Networks** has served as the NFL's Official Wi-Fi Solutions Provider for **12 consecutive seasons**, with networking solutions deployed in 28 of 31 NFL stadiums. NFL CIO Gary Brantley has stated: "Connectivity is the modern-day 12th man in any stadium."

The Ravens' experience directly illustrates the technology-satisfaction link. Their $120M investment produced a #1 ranking in stadium technology and Wi-Fi, directly measured through the VOF. The Buccaneers cited their 5G wireless deployment, cashless transactions, and mobile ticketing as drivers of their sustained VOF dominance.

## How other leagues measure fan satisfaction

The NFL is not alone in systematically measuring fan sentiment. The **NBA** operates its own "Voice of Fan" program — nearly identically named — collecting over **100,000 responses** across the NBA App, League Pass, NBA ID, NBA.com, and in-venue channels in the 2024–25 season. It uses NPS and Overall Satisfaction (OSAT) as headline metrics, with AI analytics partner Dimension Labs processing open-ended responses in near real-time (reducing insight delivery from 60 days to under 6 hours). The NBA's program is broader than the NFL's in its coverage of digital touchpoints.

**MLB** runs the "Voice of the Consumer" (VOC) program — a two-part survey that contacts fans both after ticket purchase and after game attendance, capturing the full journey arc. The Chicago Cubs ranked #1 in both Overall Guest Experience and Staff Experience in 2023. The **NHL** distributes a Ticket Buyer Trends Study administered by **Qualtrics**, linking survey responses to actual ticket purchase data — a methodological feature that enables richer behavioral analysis. The **Premier League** uses an independent research firm (Yonder) to conduct an annual Matchday Fan Experience Survey across all 20 clubs, measuring 33,000 attendees on 14 dimensions. MLS and the NCAA lack formal league-wide branded survey programs.

Third-party benchmarks provide the most revealing cross-league comparisons. **J.D. Power's Fan Experience Study** measured 35 metrics across 7 factors on a 1,000-point scale and found that **NFL teams scored lowest in every single market studied** in 2017, with MLS and NHL teams consistently scoring highest. The **Qualtrics/XM Institute** benchmark found the NFL has the **lowest percentage of "promoters"** (NPS) at 49%, compared to 57% for NASCAR. These findings suggest the NFL's larger, more expensive stadium environments create inherently greater friction — making the technology investment case even more compelling.

## Building the correlation between technology maturity and fan satisfaction

The evidence base for linking technology investment to fan satisfaction and revenue is substantial, though it relies more on industry data than controlled academic research.

**Deloitte's Fan Engagement Survey** (15,000+ respondents across 7 leagues) established that fans who are more satisfied attend significantly more games and spend more per visit. In the highest-satisfaction league studied (WNBA), fans attended roughly **40% more games** at every fandom level. **Evolved Experience Solutions** analyzed 25,000+ fans across four leagues and found that when fans were "exceptionally satisfied" with entertainment and customer service, they spent **up to 20% more on retail and 10% more on concessions**. Millennials spent up to 21% more when the experience was excellent, and teams saw **8% game-over-game revenue increases** from small feedback-driven adjustments.

A rigorous correlation framework would construct a **Technology Maturity Index** scored across 8 dimensions — connectivity infrastructure (Wi-Fi density, bandwidth, generation), digital commerce (cashless, checkout-free, mobile ordering, POS speed), entry/security technology (biometrics, walk-through screening), digital signage and displays, AI and personalization, data analytics platforms, wayfinding and navigation, and AR/VR experiences — then map these scores against VOF satisfaction data and revenue metrics (per-cap spending, STM renewal rates, premium seat sales, average transaction value).

The critical methodological challenge is isolating technology's contribution. Deloitte's "Core Four" attributes — compelling team, clean/comfortable/safe facility, quality sightlines, and exciting atmosphere — are **50%+ more important** than any other factor. Technology's impact is often indirect: it enables faster transactions, shorter wait times, better navigation, and richer content rather than driving satisfaction as a standalone variable. A pre/post longitudinal design measuring the same venue before and after technology upgrades, controlling for team performance and market conditions, would yield the strongest causal evidence.

The case studies are persuasive directionally. Mercedes-Benz Stadium's combination of pricing innovation and technology investment produced a **16% per-cap spending increase** and first-to-last transformation in VOF F&B rankings. The 49ers' combination of inclusive menus and mobile ordering technology at Levi's Stadium drove a **29% increase in total per-attendee spending**. The Intuit Dome's cashier-less checkout achieved **4–8x faster retail transactions** in its first six months. These real-world examples, when correlated with VOF improvement trajectories, create a compelling narrative: technology investment reduces friction, friction reduction drives satisfaction, and satisfaction drives revenue.

## Conclusion: VOF as the connective tissue between investment and outcomes

The NFL's Voice of the Fan survey is more than a customer satisfaction tool — it is the league's operational nervous system for the in-venue experience. Its granular measurement of technology-specific touchpoints (Wi-Fi, cellular, mobile ticketing, video boards) makes it uniquely suited as the dependent variable in any study correlating technology maturity with fan outcomes. Three insights emerge that have not been widely recognized.

First, the VOF's designation of Stadium Technology as "the most impactful category in determining overall fan experience satisfaction" — per the Buccaneers' characterization of NFL analysis — suggests the league already possesses internal regression data showing technology's predictive power. This data, if accessible, would be the strongest existing evidence for the technology-satisfaction link.

Second, the NFL's consistently poor performance in third-party cross-league studies (last in every J.D. Power market, lowest NPS in Qualtrics benchmarking) indicates a structural satisfaction deficit driven by large-venue complexity and high price points. Technology is the primary lever for overcoming this structural disadvantage — the only scalable way to reduce friction, personalize experiences, and increase throughput across 70,000-seat environments.

Third, the convergence of the VOF's measurement framework with emerging technology maturity models creates an immediate opportunity. By mapping a standardized technology maturity assessment against VOF category scores across 32 venues, one could produce the first comprehensive correlation analysis demonstrating that investment in connectivity, digital commerce, intelligent signage, and AI-driven personalization systematically predicts higher fan satisfaction — and that satisfied fans spend more, renew more, and advocate more. The data architecture already exists; the analysis is waiting to be built.
